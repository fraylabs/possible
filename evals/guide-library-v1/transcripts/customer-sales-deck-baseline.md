Recommended medium

Use an editable 16:9 PowerPoint deck, built with native text, shapes, image frames, and master layouts. PowerPoint is the most portable production handoff; the eventual package should include the editable .pptx and a review PDF. No file has been created or published.

Narrative direction

The arc should feel like a quiet transition: scattered digital memories → the emotional cost of rarely seeing them → a shared object in the home → a carefully qualified privacy-first proposition → an invitation to join the waitlist. Keep copy sparse, imagery tactile, and pacing calm.

Ten-slide structure

1. Cover — “Bring the photos you love back into the room.” Use the approved product name and one restrained hero image or clearly labeled concept render.

2. The scattered-photo reality — Show an editorial collage of phones, message threads, folders, and camera rolls. Avoid statistics; use observational copy such as “Our photos are everywhere, but rarely in front of us.”

3. What gets lost — Focus on the human tension: captured moments can become buried, private, or forgotten. Use one warm family image and no unsupported behavioral claims.

4. A different destination — Introduce the shift from feeds and folders to a shared physical presence. A simple conceptual diagram can move from “scattered photos” to “one shared object,” without implying unverified integrations or workflows.

5. Meet the frame — Present the privacy-first digital photo frame as an offline shared object. Use only approved product language, specifications, and visuals. Any provisional render must say “Concept image.”

6. The intended experience — Use a three-beat storyboard: gather meaningful photos, bring them into the room, revisit them together. Label the sequence “Illustrative” until the actual setup and interaction flow are confirmed.

7. Designed to live at home — Show scale, placement, and visual character in a restrained interior. Mention materials, dimensions, finishes, controls, or display behavior only when supplied and approved.

8. What “privacy-first” means — Do not fill this slide with promises. Present verified facts only. Until those exist, frame the content as questions the product must answer: What works offline? What data, if any, leaves the device? What controls remain with the household? Avoid words such as “secure,” “encrypted,” “never shared,” or “fully private” without substantiation.

9. From stored files to a shared ritual — Close the emotional argument with a quiet room-scale image and a concise line about making selected memories visible. Do not use customer quotes, adoption claims, or implied product proof.

10. Waitlist — “Bring your photos back into the room. Join the waitlist.” Include the confirmed URL, a tested QR code, and a plain-text short link. Add: “Joining the waitlist does not indicate pricing, purchase availability, or a delivery date.”

Asset handoff

The eventual designer needs an approved logo and product name; brand colors and licensed fonts; approved product photography or clearly labeled renders; licensed family and interior imagery with model/property releases where required; approved UI captures, if any; and the final waitlist URL and QR code. Maintain an asset manifest recording owner, source, license, approval status, and expiration or usage limits. Favor natural domestic scenes, diverse family structures, soft daylight, and uncluttered compositions. Avoid generic “tech lifestyle” imagery or staged reactions.

Accessibility checks

Use a logical reading order and master layouts; at least 28 pt body text where practical; strong contrast, targeting 4.5:1 for normal text; and no meaning conveyed by color alone. Add useful alt text to every meaningful image and mark decorative images accordingly. Keep copy plain, avoid text baked into imagery, provide the waitlist as both QR code and readable URL, and verify accessibility in the exported PDF as well as the source deck.

Source and claim controls

Create a claim log for every factual statement, with its source, owner, approval status, and review date. Tag unresolved content visibly as [VERIFY], [COPY TBD], or [ASSET NEEDED]. Product, offline-operation, data-handling, and privacy language requires product and legal review. Do not add pricing, launch timing, availability, testimonials, market statistics, certifications, comparative claims, or technical assurances unless directly sourced and approved.

Export and review gates

Before release, verify all text remains editable; fonts embed or substitute safely; images are high-resolution and correctly licensed; links and QR codes work; slide numbers, notes, and metadata are clean; and both 16:9 presentation mode and PDF render without clipping. Review in this order: narrative and tone, brand/design, product accuracy, privacy/legal, asset rights, accessibility, then final link and export QA. The release package should contain the .pptx, accessible review PDF, asset manifest, claim log, and a versioned approval record.

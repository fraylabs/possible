At this point, the responsible outcome is a decision-ready plan—not a customer-ready product, launch, or shipment.

What can be established now

Your current non-negotiable product principles are clear:

- Photos remain usable from local storage without a mandatory cloud account.
- Setup and everyday viewing should work without creating an account.
- The object should feel calm and furniture-like, with technology visually subordinate to the photographs.
- The intended assembly and delivery context is Singapore.
- Publishing, data collection, address sharing, quote requests, purchasing, and fabrication require separate future authorization.

I can turn those principles into a private requirements brief, an options comparison, and an evidence plan. That can cover:

- Candidate system architectures: display, compute, storage, photo-import path, controls, power, and enclosure.
- An offline-first data flow, including onboarding, photo transfer, backup, deletion, updates, and recovery.
- Industrial-design directions with measurable constraints such as overall depth, bezel treatment, visible cables, ventilation, stability, service access, and acceptable noise and light.
- A phased bill-of-materials framework listing required component categories without pretending that specific parts are compatible or available.
- Prototype and production test plans covering image quality, boot reliability, thermal behavior, power loss, storage corruption, long-duration playback, assembly, and packaging.
- Non-public website copy, wireframes, a marketing-video storyboard, and a privacy-conscious waitlist design. Product claims would be marked provisional until evidence supports them.
- Stage gates from concept selection through engineering prototype, safety and compliance review, pilot assembly, logistics validation, and only then launch.

Meaningful decisions needed from you

The highest-leverage decisions should be made in this order:

1. Product and pilot: Who is the first customer, what problem makes this frame worth buying, will customers receive a finished assembled device or a kit, and how many pilot units do you want?
2. Commercial limits: What are the target retail price, maximum per-unit parts cost, total development budget, and acceptable assembly time per unit?
3. Timing: Is there a real launch deadline, or should readiness determine the date?
4. Display experience: Preferred size range, portrait/landscape behavior, resolution, brightness, matte versus glossy surface, viewing distance, and whether touch is acceptable.
5. Photo workflow: USB, SD card, local-network upload, direct phone transfer, or some combination; desired storage capacity; supported file formats; backup expectations; and whether optional cloud features are allowed.
6. Compute behavior: Instant-on expectations, slideshow features, remote administration, software-update policy, network availability, and how the owner recovers a failed device.
7. Power and safety: External low-voltage adapter versus another design, always-on use, cable routing, accessible parts, thermal limits, child or pet exposure, and whether you will sell only finished products.
8. Physical construction: Wood, metal, polymer, or mixed materials; target dimensions and weight; prototype versus pilot manufacturing process; repairability; and the tools and workspace available for home assembly.
9. Launch package: Brand and audience, required site pages, video duration and channels, acceptable marketing claims, and what—if any—waitlist fields may eventually be collected, retained, and deleted.
10. Logistics: Pilot quantity, packaging expectations, delivery area, and import responsibilities. Your street address is not needed during concept work and should only be provided privately when a specific authorized transaction requires it.

What remains unproven

No specific display, computer, storage device, adapter, enclosure process, or supplier is currently validated. Compatibility, thermal safety, electrical safety, electromagnetic behavior, structural stability, durability, photo quality, boot time, offline reliability, update recovery, assembly time, yield, and repairability are all unknown.

Unit economics, inventory availability, lead times, Singapore import and delivery costs, applicable compliance obligations, packaging performance, warranty burden, and customer-support needs are also unverified. Likewise, demand, price acceptance, website conversion, waitlist interest, and the effectiveness of a marketing video have not been tested.

Therefore, it would be misleading to describe parts as ready to ship or units as ready for customers. The next responsible milestone is a private concept specification with explicit assumptions and pass/fail tests. Only after prototype evidence should any supplier contact, quote request, purchase, fabrication, public claim, website publication, or waitlist collection be considered—and each would still require your explicit approval.
